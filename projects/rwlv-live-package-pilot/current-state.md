# Current State

## Current Resorts World Live Experience

Resorts World Live appears to function as an entertainment discovery and conversion surface for shows, residencies, nightlife, and event-driven resort visits. The experience likely prioritizes event awareness, artist or show detail, schedules, and ticket-oriented next steps.

This pilot treats the current experience as a starting point for opportunity analysis. It does not assume the existing page is broken. The working hypothesis is that entertainment intent may not yet be fully merchandised as a broader resort stay, dining, VIP, or loyalty journey.

## Current User Journey

1. A guest discovers or arrives at a Resorts World Live entertainment surface.
2. The guest scans upcoming events, artists, dates, venues, or featured entertainment.
3. The guest selects an event or show that matches their interest.
4. The guest evaluates timing, ticket path, venue context, and practical trip requirements.
5. The guest may separately consider a hotel stay, restaurant reservation, group plan, or nightlife addition.
6. The guest either proceeds to a ticketing or booking path, leaves to research elsewhere, or returns later through another channel.

## Current Conversion Paths

Known or likely conversion paths:

- Ticket purchase or ticketing handoff.
- Room booking from nearby resort navigation or promotional paths.
- Restaurant reservation through dining surfaces or third-party reservation tools.
- Loyalty sign-in, join, or offer exploration.
- Event inquiry for groups, VIP, or special access.
- Cross-navigation into resort, rooms, dining, nightlife, or offers.

Current-state research should verify which paths exist, which are primary, and where measurement continuity is lost.

## Current Merchandising Approach

The likely current merchandising model is event-first: the show, artist, venue, or schedule is the primary object. Resort offerings may be nearby but not always framed as a coordinated guest experience.

Potential merchandising gaps to validate:

- Room + event pairing may not be presented as one coordinated decision.
- Dining + event timing may require guest self-planning.
- VIP or premium access may be present but not connected to stay or dining context.
- Seasonal or weekend framing may not unify events, rooms, dining, and loyalty under one narrative.
- Cross-sell may happen through navigation rather than curated package logic.

## Observed Strengths

- Entertainment creates a strong emotional reason to plan a visit.
- Resorts World has enough resort depth to support richer packages.
- Event dates naturally create trip-planning context.
- Premium venue, dining, room, and nightlife assets can support differentiated package narratives.
- Entertainment browsing provides high-signal behavioral intent for future personalization and packaging.

## Observed Weaknesses

- Entertainment, room, dining, and loyalty decisions may feel disconnected.
- Guests may have to assemble a complete trip manually.
- Package value may be hard to understand if inclusions, timing, and next steps are not presented together.
- Conversion paths may fragment across ticketing, room booking, dining reservations, and third-party systems.
- Analytics may undercount package interest if it only measures final booking or isolated CTA clicks.
- Mobile users may face extra friction when moving between event detail, availability, reservation, and booking surfaces.

## Research Needed

- Current page and journey audit.
- CTA inventory and destination map.
- Event detail, ticketing, room, dining, and loyalty handoff map.
- Mobile journey review.
- Baseline analytics review.
- Stakeholder validation of operational constraints.
